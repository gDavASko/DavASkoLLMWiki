A Layered Self-Validating Knowledge Base with Hybrid Retrieval for Grounding LLM Agents: Method and Empirical Evaluation on a Real Corpus

DavASko
System development and numerical experiments were carried out with the aid of AI tools (LLM assistants)
DavASko LLM Wiki framework, v3.x · Fully offline, reproducible · Русская версия

Abstract

It is verified empirically whether a curated hybrid (symbolic and semantic) retrieval layer over a knowledge base outperforms a trivial lexical file search when grounding LLM agents. On a deployed 162-document base with 15 labeled queries, recall@5, MRR and nDCG@5 are computed for the proposed engine and for baselines. Semantic retrieval yields recall@5 0.633 and MRR 0.718 against 0.333 and 0.435 for the lexical baseline; structure-aware chunking exceeds the fixed-window one by 7.8% MRR; GPU embedding gives an eight-fold speed-up. The retrieval layer is justified by measurement.

Keywords

information retrieval; knowledge base; retrieval-augmented generation; vector embeddings; ranking quality evaluation; LLM agents

1. Introduction

Relevance. LLM agents in software development increasingly operate over large and evolving project knowledge. A common grounding approach is retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) [5], in which relevant context is retrieved and supplied to the model. Yet the underlying assumption that a dedicated retrieval layer is effective is usually asserted rather than tested: it remains open whether a curated indexed knowledge layer beats the trivial alternative — direct full-text search by the agent over the files. A retrieval layer that does not beat that baseline is negative-value complexity, as it adds maintenance burden and a second, drifting copy of the truth.

Aim and objectives. The aim of this work is to establish quantitatively whether the proposed hybrid retrieval engine earns its use. The objectives are: (1) to formalize the architecture and retrieval method; (2) to build an evaluation harness with information-retrieval metrics and baselines; (3) to evaluate on a real corpus; (4) to refine the method based on observed data.

Related work. Dense retrieval maps a query and text fragments into a shared vector space and ranks by cosine similarity [4]; RAG couples such retrieval with a generator [5, 2]. The embedding model used is the multilingual Jina v3 with task-specific LoRA adapters and asymmetric query:/passage: prefixes [8], run locally via ONNX Runtime in Transformers.js [10]. Inverted-file (IVF) indexes partition vectors into clusters and probe the nearest at query time, trading recall for speed [3]. Structure-aware text splitters cut a document along its structure (headings, paragraphs) and improve fragment coherence [1]. Combining lexical and dense retrieval is established practice [2].

2. Materials and Methods

2.1 Architecture

Knowledge is organized into independent layers with a strictly downward dependency graph. Each layer separates immutable source snapshots (raw/) from derived curated pages (wiki/); both are indexed. The offline-indexing and online-query pipelines are shown in figure 1.

Figure 1 — Indexing (top) and query (bottom) pipelines

2.2 Hybrid retrieval

The symbolic stream (A) matches strict code identifiers (PascalCase of at least two parts, I* interfaces, m_* fields) against document fields; matches are weighted by field type and down-weighted by an inverse document frequency in the spirit of IDF [6]. The semantic stream (B) embeds the query with the query: prefix and ranks fragments by cosine similarity (1) [6]:

sim(q, d) = (q · d) / (‖q‖ · ‖d‖)    (1)

The streams are merged by a single effective score rather than by a hard precedence of one over the other (justification in section 4.1).

2.3 Routing and adaptive threshold

Vectors are stored one shard per layer; a shard centroid is defined as the mean of its member vectors. At query time, shards are ranked by centroid proximity and the nprobe nearest are scanned (IVF-style multi-probe [3]); when nprobe is not smaller than the number of shards, the search is exhaustive and loses no recall. Instead of a fixed cosine threshold, sensitive to a shift of the similarity distribution, a per-query adaptive threshold (2) is used:

τq = max( φ,   α · maxd sim(q, d) )    (2)

where α is a fraction of the maximum similarity (default 0.85) and φ is a lower "noise floor" (default 0.35). Construction (2) is proposed in this work as a replacement for a constant threshold.

2.4 Structure-aware chunking and provenance

The indexer tokenizes Markdown into headings, paragraphs and atomic code blocks, packs them to a target size within the [min, max] word range, hard-splits only oversized blocks, merges undersized fragments and prepends the heading path; the approach follows the idea of structure-aware splitting [1]. Code is treated as the source of truth and wiki/ pages as derived; on close scores the source is ranked higher and results are labeled primary (SOURCE) or derived (SUMMARY). Each page stores content hashes of its cited sources, which makes their divergence detectable.

2.5 Evaluation metrics

For a set of relevant documents R and a ranked result list, standard metrics are used [6, 7]. Recall in the first k (3) is the fraction of relevant documents in the top-k [6]:

recall@k = |R ∩ top-k| / |R|    (3)

Mean reciprocal rank (4) averages the reciprocal rank of the first relevant document over queries Q [6]:

MRR = (1/|Q|) · Σi∈Q 1 / ranki    (4)

Normalized discounted cumulative gain (5)–(6) accounts for the position of a relevant result [7]:

DCG@k = Σi=1..k reli / log2(i+1);   nDCG@k = DCG@k / IDCG@k    (5)–(6)

2.6 Experimental setup

The evaluation corpus is the deployed KBPro base: 162 documents (kbpro-wiki and llm-wiki layers), 433 fragments, 2 shards; the full corpus has 6 layers and 343 indexable documents. The query set comprises 15 labeled questions with required-source annotations. The model is jinaai/jina-embeddings-v3 (fp16, 1024 dimensions). The parameter top-k = 5; the threshold is adaptive (α = 0.85, φ = 0.35). The retrievers compared are semantic (dense multi-probe only), hybrid (symbols and semantics with unified ranking), flat (exhaustive flat cosine), and lexical (term overlap over the files — the "agent reads the files directly" model). The harness reuses the engine's own core functions and therefore evaluates the real engine, not a re-implementation. Embedding runs on a GPU via the DirectML provider with fallback to the CPU [9].

4. Result Discussion and Method Refinement

4.1 Hybrid ranking

The first run revealed a defect: under the original merge all symbolic matches were placed above semantic ones, whereby tangential exact matches displaced the best semantic results and the hybrid ranked worse than pure semantics (MRR 0.641 against 0.718). Two refinements, measured on the same index, removed the gap (table 2): a switch to unified score-based ranking and a tightening of symbol extraction that excludes generic acronyms. The diagnosis was localized to a single query in which the acronym "JSON" was treated as a code identifier.

4.2 Comparison of chunking strategies

With all else equal, the index was rebuilt under each strategy. Structure-aware chunking surpassed the fixed-window one in ranking at equal recall (table 3), which confirms on this corpus the common qualitative observation on the benefit of structural splitting [1].

4.3 Indexing speed

The correctness of each speed-up was verified before time was measured. Batched embedding is provably equivalent to single-item embedding (minimum cosine 0.99999997), but on the CPU it gives only about 11% gain, since a forward pass of the model saturates the computation. Moving computation to a GPU via DirectML [9] gives an eight-fold speed-up while preserving numerical equivalence of the results (table 4), reducing full-index time from about half an hour to a few minutes.

5. Discussion

The obtained data show that the dense retrieval layer is not decorative: it substantially outperforms the lexical baseline in both recall and ranking. For natural-language questions semantic retrieval is the working tool; the symbolic stream is neutral on this set but is intended to raise precision on identifier queries not present in the set. Each method improvement (section 4) was driven by measurement rather than by an a priori assumption, which illustrates the embedding of the evaluation harness into the engineering loop. With two shards the clustering mechanism does not affect the result and begins to matter only across many layers.

6. Limitations and Threats to Validity

The following limitations are established:

  • the small query set (n = 15) leads to wide confidence intervals; the numbers indicate direction rather than a precise value;
  • the results are obtained on a single corpus; generalization to other domains is untested;
  • relevance labels are a proxy (required sources of a regression set), which may bias the recall estimate;
  • in the evaluation 2 of 6 layers are indexed, and fewer distractor documents make retrieval easier than in full deployment;
  • recall@5 = 0.633 implies a loss of about 37% of relevant material in the top-5; raising recall requires not re-ranking but a larger k, query expansion, or a change of representation;
  • high indexing speed is achieved given a GPU and an appropriate runtime build, otherwise CPU fallback applies;
  • provenance makes divergence detectable but does not remove the dual source of truth between code and derived pages.

7. Conclusion

In this work an often-asserted assumption about the effectiveness of a dedicated retrieval layer is turned into a measured fact: on a real deployed knowledge base a layered hybrid retrieval engine substantially outperforms the lexical baseline (recall@5 0.633 against 0.333, MRR 0.718 against 0.435). A measurement-driven refinement loop is demonstrated: two ranking corrections (+12% MRR) and the choice of chunking strategy (+7.8% MRR) were adopted after confirmation by data, and the speed optimizations (eight-fold GPU speed-up, batching) were introduced after proven numerical equivalence. The limitations are documented and mapped to concrete directions for further experiments. All results are reproducible with the accompanying harness.
